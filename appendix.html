Fast Approximate Parallax Shadows

Figure 1. Left: effective sensitivity ρ(t) = 1/(αt) for different power-law exponents. All curves are the same 1/t shape, scaled by 1/α. Right: sensitivity per log-decade ρ(t)·t = 1/α, a constant for each α. This is the log-uniform invariant.

Figure 2. The same 1/i weight projected into ray-space at different α. At α = 0.5 (blue), the curve is steep—near-field positions receive far more weight than far-field. At α = 2.0 (amber), the curve is nearly flat. All curves converge to 1 at t = 1.

Figure 3. Discrete sample placement and weights for N = 4 at each α. Bar height shows the 1/i weight; bar position shows where that sample falls on the ray. The red shaded region is unsampled. As α increases, the first sample moves toward the ray origin and the unsampled gap shrinks.

Figure 4. Cross-section of a height field with shadow rays cast from three fragments (A, B, C) toward the light. At each sample point along the ray, the depth difference ΔH = H_sample − H_ray is measured. Fragment A is deep in shadow (large ΔH); fragment C is near the shadow boundary (small ΔH, partial shadow); fragment B is fully lit (ΔH ≤ 0 everywhere). The continuous depth-difference formulation maps these to a smooth gradient from shadowed to lit—no binary threshold is involved.

Figure 5. Distance attenuation reinforces contact hardening. The same depth difference ΔH at a near sample (i = 1, weight 1) produces a much stronger shadow contribution than at a far sample (i = 4, weight 1/4). Combined with the α = 0.5 square-root distribution, the effective attenuation follows t−2 in ray-space, matching the solid-angle falloff of real occluders. A nearby occluder casts a dark, well-defined shadow; a distant one of the same height produces only subtle darkening.
